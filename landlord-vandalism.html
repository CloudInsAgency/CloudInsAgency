Vandalism vs. Normal Wear and Tear vs. Tenant Damage

It's important to understand what is and isn't considered vandalism:

Type of Damage Covered by Vandalism? Who Pays?
Malicious Destruction
(Intentional damage to harm property)		 ‚úÖ YES - Covered Insurance pays (minus deductible)
Accidental Tenant Damage
(Unintentional damage by carelessness)		 ‚ùå Usually NO Tenant's security deposit or liability

‚ö†Ô∏è Proving Vandalism vs. Accidental Damage

Insurance companies need proof that damage was intentional/malicious:

Clear Vandalism (Easily Covered):
‚Ä¢ Holes deliberately punched in walls (fist/foot shaped)
‚Ä¢ Windows smashed from inside
‚Ä¢ Graffiti and spray paint
‚Ä¢ Fixtures ripped out or destroyed
‚Ä¢ Deliberate water damage (pipes cut, toilets clogged)
‚Ä¢ Obvious malicious intent

Questionable Cases (May Need Documentation):
‚Ä¢ Damage that could be accidental vs. intentional
‚Ä¢ Claims require photos, police reports
‚Ä¢ May need expert assessment
‚Ä¢ Document condition before tenant moved in
‚Ä¢ Move-out inspection reports critical

What Vandalism Coverage Does NOT Include

‚ùå Typically NOT Covered Under Vandalism:

  • Tenant's belongings: Tenant needs renters insurance for their property
  • Normal wear and tear: Aging carpets, paint fading, worn fixtures
  • Accidental damage: Tenant accidentally breaks something
  • Damage you cause: Your own property damage
  • Neglect: Damage from lack of maintenance
  • Mysterious disappearance: Items missing without evidence of theft
  • Rodent/insect damage: Unless vandals deliberately introduced pests
  • Acts of God: Storm, earthquake, flood (need separate coverage)

Preventing Vandalism

While coverage protects you financially, prevention is always better:

During Tenancy:

  • Screen tenants carefully (background, references, credit)
  • Maintain good landlord-tenant relationships
  • Address maintenance issues promptly
  • Regular property inspections (per lease terms)
  • Document property condition throughout tenancy
  • Address lease violations immediately

During Evictions:

  • Follow legal eviction procedures exactly
  • Never use "self-help" evictions (provokes retaliation)
  • Remain professional and calm
  • Have sheriff/marshal handle physical eviction
  • Be present during eviction if safe
  • Change locks immediately after tenant leaves

Vacant Properties:

  • Install visible security system and cameras
  • Motion-sensor lighting on exterior
  • Secure all entry points (windows, doors)
  • Board up if extended vacancy expected
  • Regular property checks (weekly minimum)
  • Neighbor notification (ask them to watch property)
  • Keep lawn maintained (property looks occupied)
  • Remove valuable components if extended vacancy
  • Consider property sitter or periodic occupancy

Post-Move-Out:

  • Change locks immediately
  • Inspect property thoroughly right away
  • Document condition with photos/video
  • Secure property quickly
  • Begin repairs/cleaning immediately
  • Don't leave property vulnerable between tenants

Pursuing Tenants for Vandalism Damage

Your Options After Vandalism:

  • Security deposit: Apply to damages (usually insufficient)
  • Insurance claim: File with your landlord policy
  • Sue tenant: Small claims or civil court for damages
  • Criminal charges: Vandalism is a crime, file police report

Reality of Recovering from Tenants:

  • Most tenants who vandalize have no assets
  • Winning judgment doesn't mean you collect
  • Collection is difficult and expensive
  • Many landlords never recover damages from tenants
  • This is why vandalism insurance coverage is essential
