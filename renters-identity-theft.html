Types of Identity Theft Covered

Identity theft protection typically covers various types of identity fraud:

Financial Identity Theft:

  • Credit card fraud: Opening cards in your name
  • Bank fraud: Draining accounts, opening new ones
  • Loan fraud: Auto loans, mortgages in your name
  • Check fraud: Forging or cashing checks
  • Wire fraud: Electronic theft from accounts

Criminal Identity Theft:

  • Someone arrested using your identity
  • Criminal record attached to your name
  • Warrants issued for crimes you didn't commit
  • Driver's license issues from other's violations

Medical Identity Theft:

  • Someone receiving medical care in your name
  • False medical records contaminating yours
  • Insurance fraud using your information
  • Prescription fraud with your identity

Tax Identity Theft:

  • Fraudulent tax returns filed in your name
  • Someone claiming your refund
  • Employment fraud using your SSN

Employment Identity Theft:

  • Someone working using your Social Security number
  • False employment history
  • Tax problems from unreported income

Child Identity Theft:

  • Someone using your child's clean SSN
  • Damage to child's credit before they're adults
  • May not discover until college age

Common Ways Identity Theft Occurs

Understanding how identity theft happens helps you protect yourself:

Data Breaches:

  • Large companies get hacked (Equifax, Target, etc.)
  • Your personal information stolen in bulk
  • Sold on dark web to criminals
  • You may not know for months or years

Phishing Scams:

  • Fake emails/texts appearing to be from banks
  • Links to fake websites that steal login credentials
  • "Urgent" messages pressuring immediate action
  • Very sophisticated and hard to detect

Mail Theft:

  • Stealing mail from apartment mailboxes
  • Credit card offers, bank statements stolen
  • Especially risky in multi-unit buildings
  • Identity thieves open accounts using stolen offers

Public WiFi:

  • Unsecured networks at coffee shops, airports
  • Hackers intercept data on open networks
  • Login credentials and financial info stolen
  • Never access banking on public WiFi

Social Engineering:

  • Criminals call pretending to be from bank/IRS/SSA
  • Trick you into revealing personal information
  • Very convincing and official-sounding
  • Prey on fear and urgency

Lost/Stolen Wallet:

  • Driver's license, credit cards, SSN card stolen
  • Criminals can open accounts with physical ID
  • Report loss immediately to minimize damage

Protecting Yourself from Identity Theft

While coverage helps with recovery, prevention is better:

Secure Your Mail:

  • Use locked mailbox if possible (apartment buildings)
  • Opt for paperless statements (banks, credit cards)
  • Shred financial documents before discarding
  • Hold mail when traveling

Monitor Your Credit:

  • Check credit reports annually (annualcreditreport.com - FREE)
  • Sign up for credit monitoring services
  • Review bank/credit statements monthly
  • Set up transaction alerts

Protect Personal Information:

  • Never carry Social Security card in wallet
  • Don't share SSN unless absolutely necessary
  • Be skeptical of unsolicited calls/emails
  • Never click links in suspicious emails
  • Use strong, unique passwords for each account
  • Enable two-factor authentication everywhere

Secure Your Devices:

  • Use antivirus/anti-malware software
  • Keep software and apps updated
  • Don't use public WiFi for banking
  • Use VPN when on public networks
  • Lock your phone and computer

Be Cautious with Sharing:

  • Limit personal info on social media
  • Don't post birthdate, address, phone publicly
  • Scammers use social media to gather info
  • Review privacy settings regularly

Freeze Your Credit:

  • Consider credit freeze (prevents new accounts)
  • FREE at all three credit bureaus
  • Can temporarily unfreeze when needed
  • Best protection against credit fraud

Signs Your Identity May Be Stolen

Watch for these warning signs:

  • Unexpected credit card/loan denials
  • Bills or collection notices for accounts you didn't open
  • Charges on credit card you didn't make
  • Missing mail or credit card offers
  • IRS notification of multiple tax returns
  • Medical bills for treatment you didn't receive
  • Credit report shows accounts you didn't open
  • Calls from debt collectors about unknown debts
  • Unable to file taxes (someone already filed)
  • Unfamiliar hard inquiries on credit report
